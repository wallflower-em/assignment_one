Project Idea

Overview

For my project, I want to create a website that assists in finding sensory-friendly places for those with autism or other neurodivergence. Places like Woolworths, Coles and Kmart have introduced quiet hours, where they turn down the lights, lower the music and reduce the number of over-speaker announcements. The website would include a map with locations highlighted that are sensory-friendly, similar to the feature google maps offers with locating cafes, restaurants and public places. Along with the map feature, you would have the opportunity to submit locations and rate the ones on how sensory-friendly they were, similar to yelp or google reviews.

Motivation

According to the abs, as of 2015, 164,000 people with autism were living in Australia. This number increased by 42% from 2012. It has been shown that people with autism often experience sensory burnout when they constantly have to face over-stimulated environments like supermarkets and restaurants. An app like this would assist people with autism to ensure that they can better manage their social experiences and decrease the amount of time they experience sensory burnout. Sensory issues can easily be met without disturbing the neurotypical sense of normality. Things like changing the type of lighting from fluorescent to something a little less harsh, turning down music, and not using fragrance smells.

Description

People with autism commonly have both hypersensitivity and hyposensitivity. Hypersensitivity can be experienced by exposure to bright lights, sounds, smells and even textures. Hyposensitivity can be displayed in the constant need for movement, attraction to loud sounds, bright lights and vibrant colours. If a person with autism is constantly in contact with their sensitivities, it can result in sensory overload, hindering their ability to cope. It may result in feelings of intense anxiety and may result in them isolating themselves from friends, family, and the general community. Sensory-friendly venues would have to meet requirements like no harsh fluorescent lighting, turning down or not having music playing, reducing voice-over announcements, and not having excess clutter on the store like stock waiting to be put away. The website would provide a place where users could rate places based on how sensory-friendly a place is, selecting a score out of 5 for lighting, music and general ambience. Like that seen in an app that the SA government has recently released called Pavely, Pavely gives the option for the public to rate public areas, venues and shops on how disability-friendly they are. In addition to the rating system, people would be able to comment advising on the positives and negatives of the venue. A review section would hopefully provide feedback to the venue's owner and make them more aware of the needs of people with autism and sensory sensitivities. I picture this idea as a website first, then evolving into IOS and Android applications later. The general design would be like that seen on googles maps, where you can click on restaurants in your area, and it will highlight them on a map. I would like it to highlight venues catering to sensory-sensitive people and have a separate category for places with designated "sensory hours" that are sensory-friendly, like Woolworths and Kmart. A website like this would make it easier for those with autism or other sensory-sensitive disabilities to navigate their daily lives and reduce stress and anxiety when trying to complete a simple task that neurotypical people do easily. The Australian institute of health and welfare reports that many National Disability Agreement (NDA) service users with autism reported having other disabilities. 33% of users with autism reported having an intellectual disability, 8% a psychiatric disability, 7% a specific learning disability or attention deficit disorder, 6% a speech disability, 5% a physical disability and 4% a neurological disability. Due to this, the design for the website and future applications would focus on providing an accessibility-friendly website. Ensuring that the correct heading structure is used, the reason for this is because screen readers can use heading structure to navigate content. Additionally, taking into consideration the colours used in the branding and theme of the website. The most common form of colour deficiency is red/green colour deficiency. Additionally, people with learning difficulties can use colour to distinguish between different content. To ensure that both groups' needs are met, we can use white to break up the blocks of the colour.
